Brief History


The beginnings of Bristol start back during the Anglo Saxon times. Originally called Brigstowe, Bristol was initially a settlement around a wooden bridge that crossed the River Avon. In the middle ages trading became more popular and so did Bristol. Boats would come in and out of the docks with wool, wine, fish and iron.

However Bristol would to its shame become heavily involved in the slave trade during the 1700s. Bristol, during this time grew to become England’s second biggest city. Many goods were exchanged that were manufactured in Bristol for people from West Africa. The people were then sold in slavery, mainly in the Caribbean.

In the 19th century, commerce and population grew immensely in Bristol. This was in part due to a new “Floating Harbour” and Isambard Kingdom Brunel designing the Great Western Railway between London and Bristol. Brunel also designed and built the Clifton Suspension Bridge which still stands today and is a great tourist attraction for visitors.

Modern Bristol


Bristol in modern times is a bustling hub of business, education, diversity and culture! Currently the city has a population of around 459,000, with 54,000 of those being students. Bristol celebrates diversity and has 45 religions, 187 countries of birth and 91 main languages spoken! We truly are breaking the mould of being “slave traders”!

Bristol celebrates progress also. It has over 400 parks and gardens and was the first UK City to be awarded the European Green Capital in 2015. Bristol is also the Vegan Capital according to foodie website Chef’s Pencil. The city also boasts being the UK’s first ever cycling city!

Not many places can boast the beginnings of an art sensation but Bristol can. Banksy is from here and his art started a cultural wave of street art which extended from Bristol to LA to Tokyo. Aardman Animations is based in Bristol and has produced the Wallace and Gromit series. A whopping 25% of all environmental documentaries are made in Bristol, many including the legendary David Attenborough!
